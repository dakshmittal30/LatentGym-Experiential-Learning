1 · How frontier models fail

We evaluate Claude Sonnet 4.6, GPT-4o, and Gemini 2.5 Flash. Even under simple latents, they fail to adapt across tasks — in three recurring ways.

neglect

Adaptation neglect

Models restart from scratch, solving each task independently without looking for reusable structure.

breakdown

Adaptation breakdown

Models notice structure but fail to act on it reliably — they propose a hypothesis, then keep running binary search anyway.

miscalibration

Adaptation miscalibration

Given the latent explicitly, models can do worse — over-applying the rule and violating the task's constraints.

3 · Where does the lift come from?

Using the agent-switching experiment, we separate the agent that builds the cross-task history from the one that acts on it. On Number Guessing, Cross-Task RL is a better explorer and a better exploiter than Single-task RL — but the exploitation gap dominates (roughly 2–30×).

Table 8 — Exploration vs. exploitation efficiency (Number Guessing)

Relative tail-return advantage of Cross-Task RL over Single-task RL at switch point K, holding the other role fixed at reference X.

Exploration efficiency rises as K grows (the explorer has more turns to gather evidence), while exploitation efficiency falls (the exploiter has fewer tasks left to act on).

4 · Algorithmic design choices

Holding the reward fixed, we vary the feedback and prompt used during training, then evaluate across all deployment conditions. Counterintuitively, training under sparser (standard) feedback transfers more robustly than richer feedback — driven by robustness to the eval-feedback shift, not faster learning.
